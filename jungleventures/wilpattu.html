Wilpattu National Park

Nestled along the captivating southeastern coast of Sri Lanka, Yala National Park beckons nature enthusiasts and wildlife aficionados alike. Covering an expansive 97,800 hectares, it stands as the second-largest national park in the country, embracing a mosaic of ecosystems that range from dense forests to sprawling grasslands and coastal lagoons. What sets Yala apart is its reputation as a haven for biodiversity, notably housing a thriving population of Sri Lankan leopards, drawing global attention for unparalleled leopard sightings. Beyond the elusive big cats, the park is a sanctuary for a diverse array of wildlife, offering visitors a mesmerizing glimpse into the untamed wonders of the natural world. Embarking on guided safaris, visitors not only revel in the sheer beauty of Yala's landscapes but also contribute to the ongoing conservation efforts dedicated to preserving this extraordinary habitat.

Introduction to Wilpattu



Welcome to the lush wilderness of Wilpattu National Park (WNP). Ranging over 131 thousand hectares, it is Sri Lanka’s largest and one of the oldest protected areas. The park consists of five blocks declared between 1938-1973 under the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance and is managed by the Department of Wildlife Conservation. WNP spans across the Puttalam and Anuradhapura districts, and borders the Mannar and Vavuniya Districts. The area is one of the most important elephant habitats in the country and is also a great place to observe the elusive Sri Lankan leopard and the sloth bear. This park has a denser forest cover unlike many other National Parks in Sri Lanka. WNPs landscape comprises of dry zone forests and thorny scrub interspersed with extensive open plains, sand dunes and the unique Villu wetlands. Bare reddish cliffs rising abruptly from narrow beaches of the Portugal Bay and Dutch Bay are another striking feature of its landscape. WNP is in the proximity to the ancient historical city of Anuradhapura and houses several ancient ruins and artifacts from various periods of history.

History & Places of Interest



Archeological structures scattered around the park including tanks, Buddhist monasteries, stupas, and rock inscriptions, give evidence of a civilization based on irrigated agriculture that was present in the region. Thambapanni, where King Vijaya supposedly landed in about 500 BC and founded the Sinhalese people, is said to be in WNP between Kudiramale point and Moderagam Aru river mouth. Galbendi Neeravia, located North-East of Maradanmaduwa tank is supposed to be the place where Prince Saliya, the son of King Dutugemunu, lived with his bride Asokamaala 2,000 years ago. Further, prehistoric sites of the Paleolithic and Mesolithic period have also been recorded.
